Diamond

Diamond is a gemstone formed by cutting a raw diamond. Diamonds have high monetary value as one of the best-known and most sought-after gems, and they have been used as decorative items since ancient times.

Hardness

The hardness of diamond and its high dispersion of light—giving the diamond its characteristic "fire"—make it useful for industrial applications and desirable as jewelry. Diamonds are such a highly traded commodity that multiple organizations have been created for grading and certifying them based on the "four Cs", which are color, cut, clarity, and carat. Other characteristics, such as presence or lack of fluorescence, also affect the desirability and thus the value of a diamond used for jewelry.

Characteristics

The most familiar usage of diamonds today is as gemstones used for adornment—a usage which dates back into antiquity. The dispersion of white light into spectral colors is the primary gemological characteristic of gem diamonds. In the twentieth century, gemologists have developed methods of grading diamonds and other gemstones based on the characteristics most important to their value as a gem. Four characteristics known informally as the "four Cs" are now commonly used as the basic descriptors of diamonds: carat, cut, color, and clarity. This system was developed by Gemological Institute of America in 1953 as internationally recognized standard to evaluate diamonds' characteristics.

Most gem diamonds are traded on the wholesale market based on single values for each of the four Cs; for example knowing that a diamond is rated as 1.5 carats (300 mg), VS2 clarity, F color, excellent cut round brilliant, is enough to reasonably establish an expected price range. More detailed information from within each characteristic is used to determine actual market value for individual stones. Consumers who purchase individual diamonds are often advised to use the four Cs to pick the diamond that is "right" for them.

Weight

The carat weight measures the mass of a diamond. One carat is defined as 200 milligrams (about 0.007 ounces avoirdupois). The point unit—equal to one one-hundredth of a carat (0.01 carat, or 2 mg)—is commonly used for diamonds of less than one carat. All else being equal, the price per carat increases with carat weight, since larger diamonds are both rarer and more desirable for use as gemstones.

List of Other Rare Gemstones

  • Painite
    • Discovered: 1956
    • Location: Ohngaing, Myanmar
    • Named after: British gemologist Arthur Charles Davy Pain
    • Notes: Once considered the rarest mineral on Earth
  • Tanzanite
    • Discovered: 1967
    • Location: Northern Tanzania
    • Notes: Receives vibrant blue from being heated; supply may decline in next 30 years, rarer than diamonds
  • Hibonite
    • Discovered: 1956
    • Location: Madagascar
    • Named after: French geologist Paul Hibon
    • Notes: Gem-quality hibonite has only been found in Myanmar
  • Red Beryl (Bixbite)
    • Discovered: 1904
    • Location: Near Beaver, Utah, USA
    • Named after: American mineralogist Maynard Bixby
  • Jeremejevite
    • Discovered: 1883
    • Location: Russia
    • Named after: Pawel Wladimirowich Jeremejew (1830–1899)
  • Chambersite
    • Discovered: 1957
    • Location: Chambers County, Texas, USA
    • Notes: Named after the location of the deposit
  • Taaffeite
    • Discovered: 1945
    • Named after: Irish gemologist Count Edward Charles Richard Taaffe
  • Musgravite
    • Discovered: 1967
    • Location: Musgrave Mountains, South Australia
    • Notes: Named for the location
  • Black Opal
    • Location: New South Wales, Australia
    • Notes: Rarest type of opal; darker composition with a variety of colors
  • Grandidierite
    • Discovered: 1902
    • Location: Tuléar Province, Madagascar
    • Discovered by: Antoine François Alfred Lacroix
    • Named after: French naturalist Alfred Grandidier (1836–1912)
  • Poudretteite
    • Discovered: 1965
    • Location: Poudrette Quarry, Canada
    • Named after: The Poudrette family (owners of the quarry)
  • Serendibite
    • Discovered: 1902
    • Location: Sri Lanka
    • Discovered by: Sunil Palitha Gunasekera
    • Named after: Serendib, the old Arabic name for Sri Lanka
  • Zektzerite
    • Discovered: 1968
    • Location: Kangaroo Ridge, Washington, USA
    • Discovered by: Bart Cannon
    • Named after: Mathematician and geologist Jack Zektzer
